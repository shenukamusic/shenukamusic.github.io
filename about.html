SHENUKA JAYASINGHE

Shenuka is a classical pianist from Sri Lanka and currently studies under Prof. Murray McLachlan in the Royal Northern College of Music in Manchester, UK. He is an enthusiast of classical music improvisation and believes we all have the power to be creative in art. His YouTube channel is dedicated to bringing out and inspiring the creative spirit within each one of us, by improvising on anyone's ideas on the piano.

PIANIST

As a lover of the Romantic Classical himself, some of the audience's favorites are usually performances of Chopin's and Rachmaninoff's works. His repertoire includes works from J.S. Bach's Well Tempered Clavier, selected Beethoven Piano Sonatas, Prokofiev Piano Sonata No.5, selected Chopin Etudes and Ballade No.2, Rachmaninoff's Morceaux de fantaisie, Grieg Piano Concerto and other post-modern compositions such as Pablo Sonnaillion's "The Joy of Breathing" and Helen Seddon-Gray's "Shahana".

YOUTUBE CHANNEL

Shenuka is a strong believer in the fact that in our pursuit for happinness there are two positive actions that can give us a strong sense of fulfillment and happiness. That is to love and to create.

He started his YouTube Channel to inspire the creative spirit that's within us all to create music. This creativity is especially apparent within children, where they do not care about what the world may think of their innovations or lack thereof; they just do what makes them happy. There is a lot that we can learn from them, and perhaps the channel may inspire someone to value the creative power inside them.

ENSEMBLE DIRECTOR

Shenuka is the director of The Templars Ensemble. It is the only one of its kind in the UK, where they are newly bringing in (or technically because of its ancient history, brining back) the niche art of Corsican liturgical chant. It is unknown where the style would have originated, but because this tradition was well preserved by the Corsicans, the name is upheld by them. There are similar sounds to this chant that can be heard in Georgian chant, Greek-Byzantine chant, Mozarabic chant and even Sri Lankan and Korean folk music. It is a universal sound of transedence that The Templars Ensemble hope to bring the cosmopolitan city of Manchester.
